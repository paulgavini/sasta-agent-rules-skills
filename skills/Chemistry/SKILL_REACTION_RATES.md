# Skill: Reaction Rates

## Purpose

This skill allows the agent to explain, teach, calculate or design tasks about chemical reaction rates using suitable collision theory, variables, graphing and evidence.

## When To Use

Use this skill when the user asks for:

- reaction rates explanations
- factors affecting rate
- collision theory
- rates practicals
- rate graphs or calculations
- catalyst, concentration, temperature or surface-area tasks
- SACE Chemistry reaction rates support

## Inputs

- year level or SACE stage
- reaction or context
- factor being investigated
- data table, graph or practical setup
- required calculation or explanation
- safety and equipment constraints
- whether the output is student-facing or teacher-facing

## Process

1. Identify the rate concept: factor, measurement, graph, mechanism or calculation.
2. Define rate as change in amount, concentration, mass, volume or another observable per unit time.
3. Connect factors to particle collision ideas at the appropriate level.
4. Keep one independent variable clear in practical designs.
5. Include units for rate, such as `cm3/s`, `g/min` or `mol L^-1 s^-1`.
6. Interpret graphs by checking axes and gradients.
7. Distinguish faster rate from greater yield or greater equilibrium amount.
8. Include safety and classroom realism for practical tasks.
9. Avoid overclaiming mechanisms beyond supplied evidence.
10. Add common mistakes where useful.

## Quality Criteria

- The rate measure is clear.
- Variables and units are correct.
- Collision theory is age-appropriate.
- Graph interpretation uses axes and units.
- Practical advice is safe and realistic.
- Rate, yield and equilibrium are not confused.

## Failure Modes

- Confusing rate with total amount produced.
- Ignoring units on rate graphs.
- Changing more than one variable in a practical.
- Saying catalysts are used up.
- Treating collision theory as more detailed than the course requires.
- Omitting safety notes for gas-producing or acid reactions.

## Completion Checklist

- Is the rate being measured clear?
- Are variables and controls identified?
- Are units correct?
- Is the explanation linked to evidence?
- Are graph and practical limitations addressed?
- Are safety and local checks visible?
