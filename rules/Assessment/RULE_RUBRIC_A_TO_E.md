# Rule: Rubric A To E

## Purpose

Ensure A-E rubrics describe quality of evidence clearly, fairly and consistently, using standards-based language rather than vague judgement words.

## Applies To

- Australian Curriculum F-10 rubrics
- SACE Stage 1 A-E grade rubrics
- task-specific marking guides
- feedback and grade explanations
- moderation support materials

## Rule Statement

The agent must create A-E rubrics that describe observable differences in performance against criteria, aligned to the relevant standards and task evidence.

## Requirements

- Align criteria to supplied standards, achievement standards or performance standards.
- Describe evidence quality, not student traits.
- Use clear progression from A to E without simply changing adjectives.
- Keep descriptors specific to the task and subject.
- Avoid percentage-only grade descriptions unless the user supplies a marking scheme.
- Do not imply exact SACE grade boundaries unless official or school-approved information is supplied.
- Keep each descriptor concise enough for practical marking.
- Avoid assessing content not required by the task.
- Include enough detail to support consistent judgement.

## Decision Logic

Use A-E rubrics when the user needs graded performance levels. If the task is formative or the user wants strengths and next steps, consider a single-point rubric instead. If the user supplies SACE performance standards, adapt their language carefully and keep task-specific evidence visible.

## Examples

- An A descriptor explains what high-quality evidence includes, such as accurate analysis, justified conclusions and effective use of data.
- A C descriptor describes satisfactory evidence aligned to the expected standard.
- An E descriptor identifies minimal or missing evidence without insulting the student.
- A criterion about investigation design focuses on variables, controls and method quality.

## Non-Examples

- A: excellent, B: good, C: satisfactory, D: limited, E: poor.
- Rubric levels based on effort or neatness when not assessed.
- Generic descriptors that could fit any subject.
- Grade boundaries invented from no supplied marking scheme.
- Criteria that duplicate each other and double-count the same evidence.

## Quality Check

- Are criteria aligned to standards and task evidence?
- Do descriptors show meaningful progression?
- Are descriptors evidence-based and student-neutral?
- Is SACE language used accurately where relevant?
- Is the rubric practical to mark with?
- Are invented grade boundaries avoided?
