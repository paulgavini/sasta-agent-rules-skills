# Rule: Parent Facing Language

## Purpose

Ensure communication with parents and carers is clear, respectful, factual and focused on supporting the student's learning and wellbeing.

## Applies To

- parent and carer emails
- report comments
- assessment explanations
- behaviour or learning updates
- meeting invitations and follow-ups
- LMS announcements visible to families

## Rule Statement

The agent must write parent-facing communication in plain, respectful language that strengthens school-family partnership and avoids unnecessary jargon, blame or disclosure.

## Requirements

- Use `parents and carers` or the user's preferred school wording.
- Explain school or assessment terms in plain language.
- Focus on evidence, support and next steps.
- Avoid deficit language about students, families or backgrounds.
- Avoid teacher-only acronyms unless explained.
- Protect student and staff privacy.
- Use a calm and factual tone for concerns.
- Invite appropriate follow-up when the matter needs conversation.
- Keep messages concise while including the information families need.
- Do not imply a diagnosis, motive or home circumstance without supplied evidence.

## Decision Logic

If the message is about progress, include what the student has shown and what will help next. If the message is about a concern, state the concern factually and propose a practical next step. If the issue is sensitive, suggest review by the teacher or leader before sending.

## Examples

- "Ari has submitted the first part of the investigation and now needs to add a conclusion that uses data from the table."
- "I am writing to organise a time to discuss how we can support Maya with the upcoming assessment."
- "The task is due on Friday 24 May. Students should submit their graph and written explanation through Daymap."

## Non-Examples

- "Your child is lazy."
- "They have no understanding of the topic" when only one task is available.
- Unexplained acronyms such as LAP, ADC or SHE in a family email.
- Mentioning another student's behaviour or grade.
- Turning a simple update into a long policy explanation.

## Quality Check

- Is the message plain and parent-friendly?
- Is the tone respectful and partnership-focused?
- Are evidence and next steps clear?
- Is privacy protected?
- Are acronyms and school terms explained?
- Is the message concise enough for the purpose?
