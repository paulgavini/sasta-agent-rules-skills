# Rule: Physics Notation

## Purpose

Ensure physics notation is clear, consistent, age-appropriate and aligned with the supplied task, course or formula convention.

## Applies To

- formulas and worked examples
- graph analysis
- practical reports
- physics assessment tasks
- answer keys and marking guides
- SACE Physics support materials
- equation formatting in Word or Markdown

## Rule Statement

The agent must use physics symbols, subscripts, vectors, signs, units and variable definitions consistently, and must avoid changing notation in ways that confuse students or alter meaning.

## Requirements

- Define symbols when students may not know them.
- Preserve supplied notation from the user, task sheet, formula sheet or official course material.
- Use standard physics symbols where appropriate, such as `v`, `u`, `a`, `t`, `F`, `m`, `E`, `p`, `V`, `I` and `R`.
- Distinguish scalar and vector quantities when direction matters.
- Use signs consistently for direction, displacement, velocity, acceleration and force.
- Preserve subscripts and superscripts in editable form.
- Use clear notation for changes, such as `delta v` or `Delta v`, when symbols may not render.
- Avoid using one symbol for two different quantities in the same worked example.
- Keep notation at the right level for the year level or SACE stage.
- Do not invent formula-sheet notation when official or supplied notation is required.

## Decision Logic

If the user supplies a notation convention, follow it. If none is supplied, use common school physics notation and define variables. For senior or SACE work, check current subject materials or supplied formula sheets where the exact convention matters.

## Examples

- `v` = final velocity, `u` = initial velocity, `a` = acceleration, `t` = time.
- `Delta x` = change in position or displacement, depending on the stated convention.
- A negative velocity indicates motion in the chosen negative direction, not necessarily slowing down.
- `F_net` is clearer than reusing `F` for several forces in one example.

## Non-Examples

- Switching between `d`, `s` and `x` for displacement without explanation.
- Treating speed and velocity as interchangeable when direction matters.
- Dropping a subscript from `E_k` and then using `E` for a different energy.
- Using `m` for both mass and metres in a way that is ambiguous.
- Copying advanced vector notation into a junior task without need.

## Quality Check

- Are variables defined?
- Is notation consistent?
- Are signs and directions explained where needed?
- Are subscripts and superscripts preserved?
- Does notation match supplied or official material?
- Is the notation appropriate for the students?
