# Rule: Assessment Structure

## Purpose

Ensure assessment tasks are clear, valid, curriculum-aligned and usable by students and teachers.

## Applies To

- formative and summative assessment tasks
- SACE school assessment and external assessment preparation
- Australian Curriculum F-10 assessment tasks
- task sheets, rubrics, marking guides and student instructions
- practical, written, oral, digital and project-based assessments

## Rule Statement

The agent must structure assessment tasks so students know what to do, what evidence to produce, how they will be judged and what conditions apply.

## Requirements

- Identify the year level or SACE stage, subject, topic and assessment purpose.
- Align the task to supplied criteria, Australian Curriculum achievement standards or SACE performance standards.
- State the student task clearly before adding teacher-facing notes.
- Include submission evidence, format, conditions, timing and allowed support where relevant.
- Make assessment criteria visible and linked to evidence students can produce.
- Avoid assessing skills or content not taught or not signalled by the task.
- Separate student-facing instructions from teacher-facing implementation notes.
- Include academic integrity, verification or process evidence requirements where relevant.
- Make the task manageable for the allocated time and marking workload.
- Flag when school approval, LAP alignment or local moderation is needed.

## Decision Logic

If the assessment is high-stakes, SACE-related or for reporting, include clear alignment, evidence and conditions. If the task is low-stakes or formative, keep the structure lighter but still make success evidence clear. If the user has supplied a rubric or criteria, use that as the assessment authority.

## Examples

- A task sheet includes context, task, evidence to submit, conditions, criteria and due date placeholders.
- A practical assessment includes what students collect, analyse and submit.
- A SACE task notes the relevant assessment type and performance standards basis.
- A formative exit ticket states what learning evidence the teacher should look for.

## Non-Examples

- Giving a broad project idea without submission evidence.
- Adding criteria that are not represented in the task.
- Mixing teacher moderation notes into student instructions.
- Calling a task SACE-ready without checking current SACE requirements.
- Creating a task that cannot be marked from the submitted evidence.

## Quality Check

- Is the assessment purpose clear?
- Are task instructions and evidence requirements explicit?
- Are criteria aligned to curriculum or standards?
- Are conditions, timing and support stated where needed?
- Can the submitted work be marked fairly?
- Are teacher notes separated from student instructions?
