# Rule: Year 8 Assessment

## Purpose

Ensure Year 8 assessment tasks are age-appropriate, curriculum-aligned, clearly scaffolded and fair for early secondary learners.

## Applies To

- Year 8 science, digital technologies and cross-curricular assessment tasks
- rubrics, marking guides and feedback
- practical investigations, projects, tests and presentations
- student-facing instructions and teacher notes

## Rule Statement

The agent must design Year 8 assessment so students can understand the task, produce the required evidence and demonstrate the expected curriculum learning without senior-level assumptions.

## Requirements

- Align the task to Year 8 Australian Curriculum content and achievement standards or supplied school criteria.
- Use clear, concrete student-facing instructions.
- Break complex tasks into stages where needed.
- Include scaffolds for planning, data, explanation or reflection when they support the intended skill.
- Assess a manageable number of criteria.
- Keep language, workload and independence suitable for Year 8.
- Avoid hidden extension expectations or senior secondary terminology unless explicitly taught.
- Provide explicit evidence requirements such as table, graph, code file, response, model, presentation or reflection.
- Make safety, device, platform and group-work expectations clear where relevant.
- Keep teacher-facing differentiation or extension notes separate from core student instructions.

## Decision Logic

If the assessment is for Year 8, pitch the core task at expected Year 8 learning and label extension separately. If a task uses senior-style features, simplify or scaffold them unless the user says the class is advanced. If the assessment contributes to reporting, align evidence to the relevant achievement standard.

## Examples

- A Year 8 practical report can scaffold question, variables, data table, graph, conclusion and evaluation.
- A coding assessment can specify required features and a short reflection, rather than expecting a full design folio.
- A research task can provide source prompts and a clear structure for using evidence.
- A rubric can use concise descriptors students can understand.

## Non-Examples

- Expecting Year 8 students to write a senior-style scientific report without scaffolding.
- Assessing university-level theory because it relates to the topic.
- Giving vague project instructions and a broad rubric.
- Making extension content part of the core grade without warning.
- Using SACE performance standards for a Year 8 task.

## Quality Check

- Is the task clearly Year 8?
- Are instructions concrete and staged where needed?
- Is the evidence students must submit explicit?
- Are criteria manageable and aligned?
- Is extension separate from core expectation?
- Are safety, platform and group-work conditions clear where relevant?
