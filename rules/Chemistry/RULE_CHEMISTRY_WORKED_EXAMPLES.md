# Rule: Chemistry Worked Examples

## Purpose

Ensure chemistry worked examples show chemical reasoning, notation, units and assumptions clearly without hiding key steps.

## Applies To

- chemical equation examples
- stoichiometry and mole calculations
- concentration and dilution calculations
- reaction rates and equilibrium support
- answer keys and marking guides
- SACE Chemistry preparation
- student tutorials

## Rule Statement

The agent must write chemistry worked examples that preserve chemical meaning and show enough reasoning for students to understand the method.

## Requirements

- State the chemical question being answered.
- Identify given quantities, required quantity and relevant equation or relationship.
- Use correct formulas, charges, states and units.
- Balance equations before using mole ratios.
- Show mole ratios from the balanced equation where relevant.
- Include units in substitutions and final answers.
- Explain assumptions, such as excess reactant, standard conditions or aqueous state, when they matter.
- Keep significant figures or precision consistent with the task.
- Interpret the final answer in context.
- Avoid adding advanced theory beyond the requested level.

## Decision Logic

For junior chemistry, focus on visible steps and simple particle or mole reasoning. For SACE work, use the supplied task, formula sheet or subject material where specific notation or evidence matters. For answer keys, be concise but still include enough evidence to justify the answer.

## Examples

```text
Balanced equation:
2H2 + O2 -> 2H2O

Mole ratio:
2 mol H2 : 1 mol O2 : 2 mol H2O
```

- Use a balanced equation before stoichiometry.
- Define `n = m / M` before substituting values.
- State whether a calculated rate is average or instantaneous when relevant.

## Non-Examples

- Using an unbalanced equation to find mole ratios.
- Omitting units from concentration or volume.
- Treating `mol` and `g` as interchangeable.
- Changing formulas to make the arithmetic easier.
- Giving an answer without the chemical relationship.

## Quality Check

- Is the chemical relationship clear?
- Is the equation balanced before ratios are used?
- Are formulas, charges and units correct?
- Are assumptions visible?
- Is the final answer interpreted?
- Is the explanation appropriate for the student level?
