# Rule: Data And Uncertainty

## Purpose

Ensure science data, measurements, graphs and conclusions are handled with appropriate attention to accuracy, precision, uncertainty, validity and reliability for the year level.

## Applies To

- practical investigations and reports
- graphing, data tables and analysis questions
- physics, chemistry and junior science worked examples
- assessment tasks, rubrics and feedback
- conclusions and evaluation prompts

## Rule Statement

The agent must treat science data as evidence with limits, and must help students distinguish measurement, variation, uncertainty and conclusion strength.

## Requirements

- Include units with measured quantities.
- Use sensible precision for instruments and year level.
- Encourage repeated measurements or trials where they improve confidence.
- Identify anomalous data carefully without deleting it without reason.
- Distinguish accuracy, precision, validity, reliability and uncertainty using language suitable for the class.
- Match graph type to the data and investigation question.
- Avoid conclusions that overstate the evidence.
- Include evaluation prompts about sources of error, limitations and improvements where relevant.
- For senior work, include uncertainty, significant figures, gradients or error discussion where appropriate.
- For junior work, keep uncertainty language concrete and linked to measurement limitations.

## Decision Logic

If students collect or interpret data, include a way to discuss data quality. If the activity is junior or introductory, use practical language such as "how much the readings varied" or "what might have affected the measurement". If the task is senior, use more formal uncertainty and validity language where appropriate.

## Examples

- A temperature table includes degrees Celsius in the header.
- A graph question asks students to describe the trend and refer to data points.
- A conclusion states "the data suggests" when there is limited evidence.
- An evaluation prompt asks how the measuring instrument affected precision.

## Non-Examples

- Reporting measurements without units.
- Treating one trial as strong evidence without comment.
- Removing an outlier without explanation.
- Claiming proof from a small or uncontrolled dataset.
- Using formal uncertainty calculations in a Year 7 task unless explicitly taught.

## Quality Check

- Are quantities labelled with units?
- Is precision suitable for the instrument and year level?
- Are repeated trials or data quality considered?
- Does graph choice match the data?
- Are conclusions proportional to the evidence?
- Are uncertainty and limitations handled at the right depth?
