# Rule: Practical Safety

## Purpose

Ensure science practical activities include appropriate safety thinking, risk controls, supervision notes and limits on what can be safely generated without local teacher review.

## Applies To

- science practical investigations
- chemistry, physics, biology, electronics and technology activities
- demonstrations, student experiments and fieldwork-style activities
- equipment lists, methods and risk notes
- teacher notes and relief lesson plans involving practical work

## Rule Statement

The agent must not present practical science activities as ready to run unless safety risks, equipment, supervision and local school approval needs are considered.

## Requirements

- Include safety notes when practical work involves chemicals, heat, electricity, glassware, biological materials, movement, tools, outdoor work or student data collection.
- Identify likely hazards and basic control measures at a teacher-usable level.
- Note required PPE, supervision, disposal, cleanup or emergency considerations where relevant.
- Keep practical tasks appropriate for the year level, setting and available equipment.
- Do not invent local school risk approvals, SDS details or official risk ratings.
- Direct the teacher to check local risk assessment systems, lab technician advice and school procedures before use.
- Avoid activities that are unsafe, illegal or unsuitable for school students.
- Provide safer alternatives where a requested activity seems risky.
- Separate student-facing method steps from teacher-facing safety notes.

## Decision Logic

If a practical activity has any foreseeable risk, include a concise teacher-facing safety section. If the risk cannot be assessed from the information available, mark the practical as needing local risk assessment before use. If a safer substitute will meet the learning goal, recommend it.

## Examples

- A chemistry practical includes PPE, disposal and teacher supervision notes.
- An electricity activity specifies low-voltage equipment and avoids mains electricity.
- A fieldwork task includes boundaries, behaviour expectations and weather considerations.
- A relief lesson avoids practical work requiring specialist supervision.

## Non-Examples

- Giving chemical quantities and method steps with no safety note.
- Claiming a practical is safe because it is common.
- Inventing SDS information or risk ratings.
- Asking students to use equipment beyond their training.
- Hiding safety notes inside student instructions where the teacher may miss them.

## Quality Check

- Are hazards and controls identified?
- Are PPE, supervision and disposal needs included where relevant?
- Is local risk assessment required before use?
- Is the practical suitable for the year level and setting?
- Are student instructions separated from teacher safety notes?
- Is a safer alternative offered where needed?
