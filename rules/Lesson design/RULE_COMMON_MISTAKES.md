# Rule: Common Mistakes

## Purpose

Ensure lessons, worked examples and feedback anticipate likely student misconceptions or errors and address them in a useful, age-appropriate way.

## Applies To

- tutorials and worked examples
- lesson plans and teacher notes
- practical investigations
- coding tasks and debugging guides
- assessment preparation
- feedback and revision materials

## Rule Statement

The agent should include common mistakes when they help teaching or learning, but must avoid inventing misconceptions as facts without checking the subject context.

## Requirements

- Focus on mistakes that are likely for the topic, year level and task.
- Explain why the mistake occurs only when useful.
- Give a correction strategy, not just a warning.
- Use neutral wording such as `common mistake` or `watch for`, not blame.
- Keep common mistakes concise.
- Include examples where they clarify the issue.
- Avoid adding too many mistakes before students have learned the core idea.
- Research or verify subject-specific misconceptions where accuracy matters.
- For teacher-facing notes, include what to look for in student work.

## Decision Logic

Include common mistakes in tutorials, revision, worked examples and teacher notes. Keep them brief in first-exposure lessons. For assessment tasks, use common mistakes in teacher notes or feedback guidance rather than giving away assessed thinking.

## Examples

- Variables: students may list the dependent variable as what they change rather than what they measure.
- Graphing: students may omit units from axes.
- Coding: students may use `=` when they need a comparison operator in some languages.
- Chemistry: students may change subscripts while balancing equations.

## Non-Examples

- Listing ten possible mistakes before the task starts.
- Saying `students always get this wrong`.
- Giving a misconception correction that is scientifically inaccurate.
- Revealing the key assessed error in a summative task prompt.

## Quality Check

- Are mistakes relevant and likely?
- Is the correction accurate?
- Is the tone neutral?
- Is the number of mistakes manageable?
- Is the placement suitable for the task type?
- Are subject-specific claims verified where needed?
