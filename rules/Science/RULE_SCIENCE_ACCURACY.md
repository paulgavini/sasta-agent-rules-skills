# Rule: Science Accuracy

## Purpose

Ensure science explanations, tasks, worked examples, diagrams, data interpretations and assessment materials are scientifically accurate, curriculum-appropriate and clear about evidence.

## Applies To

- science worksheets, tutorials and teacher notes
- practical investigations and reports
- chemistry, physics, biology and earth and space science content
- data, graphs, models and conclusions
- assessment tasks, answer keys and rubrics
- science communication for students, parents and staff

## Rule Statement

The agent must check science claims, terminology, units, models, evidence and conclusions before presenting them as teaching material.

## Requirements

- Use correct scientific terminology at the intended year level.
- Distinguish observations, measurements, inferences, models, explanations and conclusions.
- Connect claims to evidence rather than assertion.
- Avoid oversimplifications that create misconceptions.
- Use the correct units, symbols, quantities and significant figures for the context.
- Check diagrams, equations, graphs and data tables for consistency with the explanation.
- Keep models age-appropriate while noting limits where needed.
- Use official curriculum, SACE or reputable science sources when accuracy matters.
- Flag uncertainty when a scientific claim, dataset or source has not been verified.

## Decision Logic

If an error would teach a misconception, affect an assessment, create safety risk or undermine a practical investigation, verify the claim before finalising. If a simplified model is appropriate for the year level, use it but avoid wording that makes the simplification false. If senior students need more precision, add the relevant qualification.

## Examples

- State that particles are represented by models rather than implying drawings show exact reality.
- Check that a graph conclusion follows from the plotted data.
- Use `mass` and `weight` correctly in physics contexts.
- Distinguish correlation from causation when interpreting data.

## Non-Examples

- Giving a neat explanation that contradicts accepted science.
- Treating a model as literal without noting its limits.
- Mixing units or symbols inconsistently.
- Drawing conclusions that go beyond the evidence.
- Copying an unverified internet explanation into a worksheet.

## Quality Check

- Are science terms and claims correct?
- Are evidence and conclusions clearly linked?
- Are units, symbols, equations and diagrams consistent?
- Is the level of simplification appropriate?
- Are model limits noted where needed?
- Are uncertain claims checked or flagged?
