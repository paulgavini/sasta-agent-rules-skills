# Rule: Three Pass Research

## Purpose

Provide a repeatable research pattern that moves from orientation, to targeted verification, to final source selection before drafting.

## Applies To

- rule and skill drafting
- curriculum, SACE, policy and assessment research
- software and platform instructions
- evidence-informed teaching recommendations
- source-based summaries and decision support

## Rule Statement

When research is required and the task is more than a simple lookup, the agent should use three passes: orient, verify and synthesise.

## Requirements

- Keep each pass proportional to the task's risk and complexity.
- Prefer official and authoritative sources from the first pass onward.
- Record only sources that materially support the final output.
- Compare sources in the verification pass when claims matter.
- Stop researching when the source base is sufficient for the user's task.
- Do not keep searching to collect decorative citations.

## Three Pass Process

1. Orient.
   - Define the research question.
   - Identify the likely authority for the topic.
   - Find official or reputable starting sources.
   - Note useful keywords, dates, jurisdiction and limits.

2. Verify.
   - Open the most authoritative sources.
   - Check currency, authorship, purpose and jurisdiction.
   - Compare sources if the claim is high-stakes or uncertain.
   - Remove weak, outdated or irrelevant sources.

3. Synthesise.
   - Decide what the sources mean for the user's task.
   - Record the source details in the file and `SOURCE_REGISTER.md` where required.
   - Draft from the source findings, not from isolated quotations.
   - State uncertainty or limits where the source base is incomplete.

## Decision Logic

Use all three passes for curriculum, SACE, policy, assessment, safety, privacy, technical and evidence-informed tasks. Use a lighter version for low-risk drafting where one or two authoritative sources answer the question. If the first pass finds that a user-supplied document is the controlling source, focus the later passes on that document and only use external sources for context.

## Examples

- For a SACE rule, orient with SACE pages, verify the relevant subject or assessment page, then synthesise requirements into the rule.
- For a software skill, orient with the vendor documentation, verify the current feature behaviour, then write platform-specific constraints.
- For a teaching-practice rule, orient with AERO or university sources, verify the main claims, then convert them into practical classroom guidance.

## Non-Examples

- Searching broadly and drafting from snippets without opening sources.
- Stopping after the first source when the claim is high-stakes or disputed.
- Listing every source found, including unused sources.
- Repeating source language without turning it into useful guidance.
- Continuing research after the answer is already supported.

## Quality Check

- Was the research question clear?
- Were authority, currency and jurisdiction checked?
- Were weak sources filtered out?
- Was the source base sufficient but not excessive?
- Did synthesis produce practical guidance?
- Are unresolved limits stated?
