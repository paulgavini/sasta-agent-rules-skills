# Rule: Source Hierarchy

## Purpose

Ensure the agent chooses sources in an order that reflects authority, relevance, currency and risk, especially for curriculum, SACE, policy, software, safety and evidence-informed teaching claims.

## Applies To

- research before drafting rule and skill files
- curriculum and SACE alignment
- assessment, safety, privacy and AI guidance
- software, platform and technical instructions
- science, digital technologies and classroom evidence claims
- source lists, citations and source registers

## Rule Statement

The agent must prefer primary, official and authoritative sources over secondary or informal sources, and must not use weak sources to support high-stakes claims.

## Requirements

- Use the most authoritative source available for the question.
- Prefer official curriculum, SACE, government, university, professional body or vendor documentation for factual requirements.
- Use peer-reviewed or reputable education evidence sources for research-informed teaching claims.
- Use reputable secondary sources only for explanation, context or where no primary source is available.
- Do not rely on blogs, forums, social media, AI-generated text or unsourced summaries for policy, curriculum, assessment or safety claims.
- Check currency when the information may change.
- Check jurisdiction when policy, curriculum or assessment rules are local.
- Compare more than one source when accuracy matters or sources disagree.
- State uncertainty when the best available source is incomplete, old or indirect.

## Decision Logic

Choose sources in this order unless the user's supplied source is the required authority:

1. User-supplied official or school-approved material.
2. Official government, curriculum, SACE or platform documentation.
3. Professional standards, university, AERO, AITSL, eSafety or recognised expert bodies.
4. Peer-reviewed or high-quality research syntheses.
5. Reputable explanatory sources.
6. Informal sources only for leads, examples or troubleshooting, not as final authority.

## Examples

- Use SACE subject outlines and performance standards for SACE assessment requirements.
- Use Australian Curriculum pages for content descriptions and achievement standards.
- Use Microsoft documentation for current Excel Online or Power BI instructions.
- Use AERO or similar reputable sources for evidence-informed teaching strategies.

## Non-Examples

- Citing a teacher blog as the authority for SACE assessment rules.
- Using a forum answer as the only source for software instructions.
- Treating an AI summary as a source.
- Choosing an overseas curriculum page when the user asked for Australian Curriculum alignment.
- Ignoring a current official source because a secondary source is easier to read.

## Quality Check

- Is the highest-authority available source used?
- Is the source jurisdiction appropriate?
- Is the source current enough for the task?
- Are secondary sources used only for suitable purposes?
- Are weak or unsupported sources excluded from final claims?
- Are source limits or uncertainty stated?
