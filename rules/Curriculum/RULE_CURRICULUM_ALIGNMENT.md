# Rule: Curriculum Alignment

## Purpose

Ensure teaching resources, assessment tasks, rubrics, lesson plans and feedback are aligned to the correct curriculum or standards framework without inventing codes, overstating links or confusing different parts of the curriculum.

## Applies To

- Australian Curriculum F-10 resources
- SACE Stage 1 and Stage 2 resources
- assessment tasks, marking guides and rubrics
- learning intentions, success criteria and lesson sequences
- teacher notes, unit outlines and curriculum maps
- any output claiming curriculum, achievement standard, performance standard or capability alignment

## Rule Statement

The agent must base curriculum alignment on supplied criteria or official curriculum and SACE sources, and must show only alignment that is accurate, relevant and useful for the requested output.

## Requirements

- Identify the framework before aligning: Australian Curriculum, SACE, school criteria or another supplied framework.
- Use user-supplied curriculum text as the primary source when provided.
- Research or request the official source when exact wording, codes, assessment requirements or current subject specifications matter.
- For Australian Curriculum alignment, distinguish year or band descriptions, achievement standards, content descriptions, content elaborations, general capabilities and cross-curriculum priorities.
- Treat content descriptions as required learning and content elaborations as optional examples.
- Treat achievement standards as descriptions of the quality of learning students typically demonstrate, not as a list of activities.
- Link curriculum elements to evidence students will produce, not just to topic words.
- Include general capabilities only where they are genuinely developed through the learning area content.
- Keep visible curriculum mapping concise unless the user asks for a full alignment table.
- Flag uncertainty when the curriculum version, subject, year level or supplied criteria are unclear.

## Decision Logic

If the task is a student-facing resource, keep curriculum alignment teacher-facing unless the user asks students to see it. If the task is an assessment, rubric or marking guide, make the alignment visible enough for the teacher to check validity. If the task is exploratory or low-stakes, a brief alignment note may be enough. If the task could affect SACE assessment, use `rules/Curriculum/RULE_SACE_ALIGNMENT.md`.

## Examples

- A Year 8 Digital Technologies worksheet may include a brief teacher note mapping the task to the relevant content descriptions and evidence students will produce.
- A rubric should connect criteria to the intended evidence of learning rather than listing several loosely related curriculum codes.
- A lesson sequence may use achievement standards to shape success criteria, while content descriptions guide lesson content.
- A general capability should be named only when students actually practise or demonstrate that capability.

## Non-Examples

- Claiming alignment to a content description that has only a shared keyword with the task.
- Treating elaborations as mandatory teaching points.
- Adding multiple curriculum codes to make a resource look more rigorous.
- Presenting curriculum mapping to students in a way that makes the task harder to understand.
- Guessing SACE assessment requirements from memory.

## Quality Check

- Is the correct framework identified?
- Are official or supplied curriculum sources used?
- Are content descriptions, achievement standards and elaborations used correctly?
- Does the alignment match the year level, subject and task evidence?
- Are general capabilities included only where relevant?
- Is uncertainty about curriculum version or source material clearly flagged?
