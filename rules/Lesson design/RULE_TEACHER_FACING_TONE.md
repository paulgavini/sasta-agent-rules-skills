# Rule: Teacher Facing Tone

## Purpose

Ensure teacher-facing materials are practical, professional, concise and useful for planning, teaching, adapting and reviewing lessons.

## Applies To

- teacher notes
- lesson plans and sequences
- relief lesson instructions
- assessment implementation notes
- practical safety notes
- differentiation and extension guidance
- curriculum alignment notes

## Rule Statement

The agent must write teacher-facing content as practical professional guidance, not as generic theory or student-facing instruction.

## Requirements

- Use concise professional language.
- Put classroom actions, preparation and decisions where teachers can find them.
- Include timing, materials, grouping, checks for understanding and evidence collection where relevant.
- Separate teacher notes from student-facing content.
- Keep curriculum and evidence notes useful rather than decorative.
- Avoid patronising explanations of obvious teaching practice.
- State assumptions about class context, equipment or prior learning.
- Flag safety, privacy, assessment integrity or local approval needs.
- Offer options only when they help real classroom implementation.

## Decision Logic

If the teacher needs to teach from the material, include enough implementation detail. If the teacher only needs a student resource, keep teacher notes brief. If the content involves practical work, assessment or platforms, include checks that prevent classroom friction.

## Examples

- `Preparation: print one data table per student and set up one ramp per group.`
- `Check for understanding: ask students to identify the dependent variable before starting data collection.`
- `Teacher note: use the extension only after students complete the core graph.`

## Non-Examples

- Long pedagogical essays inside a lesson plan.
- Mixing teacher notes into student instructions without labels.
- Saying `differentiate as needed` with no practical options.
- Omitting safety or equipment assumptions.
- Overexplaining routine classroom actions.

## Quality Check

- Is the guidance practical for a teacher?
- Are preparation and implementation details clear?
- Are teacher notes separate from student text?
- Are assumptions and risks stated?
- Is the tone professional and concise?
- Are options useful rather than distracting?
