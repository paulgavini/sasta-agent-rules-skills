# Rule: Learning Intentions Success Criteria

## Purpose

Ensure learning intentions and success criteria are clear, useful and aligned to the lesson, curriculum and evidence of learning.

## Applies To

- lesson plans and sequences
- student-facing tutorials and worksheets
- assessment preparation lessons
- teacher notes
- LMS lesson posts
- relief lessons

## Rule Statement

The agent must write learning intentions and success criteria that help students understand the learning goal and how they can show progress, without adding unnecessary jargon or workload.

## Requirements

- Write learning intentions as what students will learn, understand or be able to do.
- Write success criteria as observable evidence students can produce or demonstrate.
- Align both to the lesson activity, curriculum or assessment evidence.
- Use student-friendly language when students will see them.
- Keep the number of success criteria manageable.
- Avoid criteria that are just task completion steps unless completion is the learning evidence.
- Avoid vague criteria such as `understand the topic` without observable evidence.
- Do not overload a lesson with every possible curriculum link.
- For assessment preparation, connect success criteria to the relevant rubric or standards.

## Decision Logic

If the lesson is student-facing, use concise `I can` or direct action language. If the lesson plan is teacher-facing, include enough precision for planning and checking learning. If the user asks for a quick activity, keep intentions and criteria brief or omit them if they would not help.

## Examples

- Learning intention: `Learn how to identify independent, dependent and controlled variables in a fair test.`
- Success criterion: `I can explain why one controlled variable helps make an investigation fair.`
- Learning intention: `Use a loop to repeat instructions in Python.`
- Success criterion: `I can write a loop that runs the same instruction a set number of times.`

## Non-Examples

- `Students will complete worksheet 4` as the only learning intention.
- `I understand science` as a success criterion.
- Five curriculum codes copied into a student slide.
- Criteria that assess content not taught in the lesson.

## Quality Check

- Is the learning goal clear?
- Can students tell what success looks like?
- Are criteria observable?
- Is the wording suitable for the audience?
- Are criteria aligned to the lesson and evidence?
- Is the list concise?
