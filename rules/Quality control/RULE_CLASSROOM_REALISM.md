# Rule: Classroom Realism

## Purpose

Ensure teaching resources, tasks and advice are practical for a real South Australian secondary classroom, not just theoretically sound on paper.

## Applies To

- lesson plans, sequences and relief lessons
- worksheets, tutorials and practical investigations
- assessment tasks, rubrics and marking workflows
- digital technologies, science and SACE resources
- teacher notes and implementation guidance

## Rule Statement

The agent must design outputs that a teacher can realistically use with the stated class, time, equipment, platform, supervision and school constraints.

## Requirements

- Respect the user's stated lesson length, class context, platform and equipment.
- Keep instructions workable for a busy teacher and understandable for students.
- Include preparation, materials, timing and supervision notes when they affect use.
- Avoid tasks that require unavailable accounts, specialist equipment or excessive setup unless the user supplied that context.
- Account for mixed ability, absences, behaviour, device issues and limited time where relevant.
- Make assessment evidence collectable and markable.
- Keep practical activities safe, supervised and feasible.
- Avoid overlong resources that cannot be completed in the available lesson time.
- Flag assumptions about class size, equipment, prior learning or school systems.

## Decision Logic

If a resource would fail because of timing, equipment, supervision, cognitive load or marking workload, adjust it before finalising. If the user has not supplied enough context, make a conservative assumption for low-risk tasks and state it. For practical, assessment or technology-dependent tasks, ask or flag what must be checked.

## Examples

- A relief lesson should work with minimal specialist teacher input.
- A practical investigation should list equipment, safety checks and what students record.
- A digital technologies task should account for account access, browser restrictions and device variation.
- A marking guide should use evidence a teacher can actually see in student work.

## Non-Examples

- Planning a 90-minute practical for a 50-minute lesson without adjustment.
- Assuming every student has the same device, software version or login.
- Creating a task with assessment evidence that cannot be collected.
- Requiring specialist equipment without noting it.
- Giving vague teacher instructions for a complex activity.

## Quality Check

- Can this be taught in the available time?
- Are materials, platforms and preparation realistic?
- Are instructions clear for students and teachers?
- Is evidence of learning visible and manageable?
- Are likely classroom constraints considered?
- Are assumptions stated?
