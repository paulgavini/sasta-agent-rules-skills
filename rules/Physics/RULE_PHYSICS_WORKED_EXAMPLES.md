# Rule: Physics Worked Examples

## Purpose

Ensure physics worked examples teach method, reasoning, units and interpretation clearly without hiding assumptions or overcomplicating the solution.

## Applies To

- student tutorials
- worksheets and revision materials
- answer keys
- assessment preparation
- graph analysis
- SACE Physics skills and applications tasks
- teacher notes

## Rule Statement

The agent must write physics worked examples that show the relevant reasoning path, not just the final calculation, while keeping the working appropriate to the students' level.

## Requirements

- Start with the physical situation or question being answered.
- List known quantities and what is being found where useful.
- Select or state the relevant model, formula or principle.
- Substitute values with units when students are learning the method.
- Rearrange formulas before substitution when this helps clarity.
- Show enough calculation steps to avoid unexplained jumps.
- Include correct units and significant figures or precision where relevant.
- Interpret the answer in context, including direction or sign when needed.
- Avoid introducing advanced derivations unless requested.
- Check that the answer matches the question, data and diagram.

## Decision Logic

Use more scaffolding for junior students, new concepts or common misconceptions. Use more concise working for senior revision or teacher answer keys. For SACE assessment preparation, align language and evidence to the task and avoid giving unauthorised assessment assistance.

## Examples

```text
Known:
d = 20 m
t = 4.0 s
Find: v

v = d / t
v = 20 m / 4.0 s
v = 5.0 m/s

The object travels at an average speed of 5.0 m/s.
```

- Use a sign explanation when velocity or acceleration is negative.
- For graphs, state what slope or area represents before calculating.
- For energy calculations, name the energy store or transfer if relevant.

## Non-Examples

- Giving only `5.0 m/s` with no method.
- Rearranging a formula incorrectly and then using the right answer by accident.
- Omitting units from every line.
- Treating a negative answer as automatically wrong.
- Adding calculus to a task intended for introductory algebra-based physics.

## Quality Check

- Is the physical question clear?
- Are knowns and unknowns identifiable?
- Is the formula or principle appropriate?
- Are substitutions and units correct?
- Is the answer interpreted in context?
- Is the working pitched to the student level?
