# Rule: Graph Interpretation

## Purpose

Ensure physics graphs are interpreted through variables, units, slope, area, shape and context rather than treated as generic maths graphs.

## Applies To

- motion graphs
- force, energy and power graphs
- practical investigation data
- graphing tutorials and worksheets
- SACE Physics tasks
- answer keys and feedback
- Tracker video analysis

## Rule Statement

The agent must interpret physics graphs by connecting graph features to physical meaning, axis units and the limitations of the data.

## Requirements

- Identify the variables and units on each axis.
- State what slope, gradient or area represents only after checking the axis quantities.
- Derive gradient and area units from the graph axes.
- Distinguish position, displacement, distance, speed, velocity and acceleration where relevant.
- Explain signs and direction where they matter.
- Interpret graph shape in context, such as constant velocity, increasing acceleration or changing force.
- Avoid assuming a straight-line relationship unless the graph or data supports it.
- Comment on outliers, uncertainty, scatter and trend quality where relevant.
- For practical data, avoid overclaiming beyond the measured range.
- For student-facing material, include a concise method for reading or analysing the graph.

## Decision Logic

If the graph is a motion graph, check axis labels before applying common rules. If the graph comes from an investigation, discuss data quality and uncertainty as well as slope or area. If the user supplies no graph, state the assumed graph type before interpreting.

## Examples

- On a position-time graph, slope represents velocity.
- On a velocity-time graph, slope represents acceleration and area represents displacement.
- On a force-distance graph, area can represent work when the context supports it.
- The gradient unit is found by dividing the y-axis unit by the x-axis unit.

## Non-Examples

- Saying `slope is always acceleration`.
- Ignoring axis units.
- Treating distance-time and displacement-time graphs as identical.
- Calling every upward curve `exponential`.
- Claiming a trend outside the measured data without caution.

## Quality Check

- Are axes and units identified?
- Is slope or area linked to the correct physical quantity?
- Are units derived correctly?
- Are signs, direction and graph shape interpreted accurately?
- Are uncertainty and data limits considered?
- Is the explanation suitable for the audience?
